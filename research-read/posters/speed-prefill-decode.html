SPEED: hide prefill KV from deep layers — 33% faster TTFT, 25% less KV memory at 128K, ~0.2-pt drop

Long-context decoders pay for prefill twice: once to compute it, again every decode step to attend over it. SPEED makes KV visibility phase-asymmetric — prefill tokens are kept only in the lower ~75% of layers, while decode tokens see the full depth. The result: 33% lower TTFT, 22% lower TPOT, 25% less active KV at 128K, with an OLMES drop from 51.4 to 51.2.

Training recipe

  • Base: decoder-only LMs evaluated at long context (up to 128K tokens).
  • Data: OLMES-style benchmark suite.
  • Compute: not reported.
  • Loss / algo: inference-time layer-asymmetric KV visibility — no training.
  • Schedule / hparams: prefill tokens visible only to the lower ~75% of layers; decode tokens full-depth.
  • Eval: OLMES average accuracy vs full-depth model; TTFT / TPOT / KV-memory profiling.

Key learnings

  • 75%-depth cutoff is the sweet spot. OLMES 51.2 vs 51.4 baseline; effectively free quality.
  • 33% TTFT, 22% TPOT speedups by skipping prefill compute and reads in upper layers.
  • 25% less active KV at 128K context — directly enlarges feasible batch / context.
  • Upper layers don't need prompt KV. Diagnostics show prompt-selection and representation stabilization happen lower.
  • Inference-only. No fine-tuning required; works on existing checkpoints.

Use when

  • Serving long-context chat or RAG where TTFT dominates UX latency.
  • Stretching context length under a fixed KV memory budget at 64K–128K.
  • Profiling which layers actually attend to the prompt — SPEED is a clean ablation knob.
